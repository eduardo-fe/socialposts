▾ Nota metodológica — Cómo se construyen los índices

1. Qué mide este monitor

Este documento mide el grado en que instituciones nominalmente independientes del Estado español han sido colonizadas por el poder político, entendiendo «colonización» como cualquier mecanismo —formal o informal— que erosiona la capacidad de una institución de actuar con autonomía respecto al Gobierno de turno. El monitor no emite juicios jurídicos ni asigna responsabilidad penal. Las valoraciones son analíticas y están basadas en hechos públicos verificables.

2. Dos métricas complementarias

Cada perfil contiene dos valoraciones distintas que miden cosas diferentes:

a) Nivel de captura institucional (decay, escala 0–3)
Mide la intensidad estructural de la politización del cargo o la institución, con independencia de quién la ocupa. Se valora sobre cuatro dimensiones:
 · Mecanismo de nombramiento: ¿existe algún proceso de validación independiente (concurso, ratificación parlamentaria por mayoría cualificada, consenso bipartidista) o el Gobierno designa libremente?
 · Perfil del titular: ¿tiene carrera política activa, militancia en un partido, cargos ministeriales previos o vínculos documentados con el partido gobernante?
 · Patrón de comportamiento institucional: ¿las decisiones de la institución coinciden sistemáticamente con los intereses del partido en el poder?
 · Precedentes acumulados: ¿el nombramiento rompe convenciones históricas, repite un patrón establecido por el mismo Gobierno o sustituye a nombramientos previamente anulados?

La escala resultante es: 0 sin evidencia de captura · 1 vínculos leves (perfil técnico con algún nexo político menor) · 2 colonización significativa (perfil claramente político o patrón de comportamiento alineado) · 3 captura clara (militancia activa, nombramiento directo sin proceso independiente y/o comportamiento sistemáticamente alineado con el Ejecutivo).

b) Índice de independencia (indScore, escala 0–100)
Mide la independencia efectiva percibida del titular concreto, ponderando factores que pueden mitigar o agravar la captura estructural. Un cargo puede estar en una institución con mecanismo de nombramiento débil (decay alto) pero haber demostrado autonomía real en sus decisiones (indScore más alto de lo que cabría esperar), o al contrario.

Los factores que reducen el indScore (más captura efectiva):
 · Militancia activa o pasada en el partido gobernante (tag-party)
 · Haber ejercido cargos ministeriales o de Gobierno (tag-minister)
 · Haber sido candidato electoral del partido (tag-candidate)
 · Nombramiento sin proceso de idoneidad externo (tag-appt)
 · Vínculos familiares o personales documentados con el poder (tag-relative)
 · Causas penales o administrativas pendientes (tag-legal)

Los factores que elevan el indScore (más independencia efectiva):
 · Perfil técnico sin carrera política previa (tag-clean)
 · Decisiones documentadas contrarias a los intereses del Gobierno que nombró (tag-assoc con contenido favorable)
 · Nombramiento con consenso bipartidista real o proceso competitivo externo
 · Continuidad en el cargo a través de cambios de signo político del Gobierno

La ponderación no es mecánica sino analítica: se valora el peso relativo de cada factor en función de la institución concreta y su mandato. Una militancia antigua y sin actividad reciente pesa menos que una vinculación ministerial activa en los meses previos al nombramiento.

3. Relación entre ambas métricas

Las dos métricas son intencionadamente independientes. El decay evalúa el sistema; el indScore evalúa al titular. En la práctica, la correlación es fuerte (decay 3 → indScore medio 9,8; decay 0 → indScore medio 81,5), pero existen casos de divergencia significativa que el monitor recoge explícitamente. Por ejemplo: una institución con mecanismo de nombramiento débil cuyo titular ha demostrado autonomía real tendrá decay 2 pero indScore 55. Esa divergencia es en sí misma información relevante.

4. Límites y sesgos del monitor

 · Sesgo de selección: el monitor cubre principalmente el período 2018–2026 (Gobiernos Sánchez). Los patrones de colonización institucional bajo gobiernos anteriores (Aznar, Zapatero, Rajoy) no se recogen con el mismo detalle, aunque se mencionan como contexto cuando son relevantes.
 · Subjetividad residual: la asignación de puntuaciones implica juicio analítico. Dos analistas razonables podrían discrepar en ±10 puntos en el indScore de un perfil concreto.
 · Información asimétrica: la opacidad de algunas instituciones (CNI, INCIBE, Enresa) hace que la evaluación sea necesariamente menos precisa que en instituciones con mayor escrutinio público.
 · Captura no es corrupción: un indScore bajo no implica que el titular haya cometido ningún delito ni que sus decisiones sean necesariamente erróneas. Mide un riesgo estructural de pérdida de independencia, no un resultado probado.
 · Actualización necesaria: los perfiles reflejan el estado de la información disponible en marzo de 2026. Los cargos, los procesos judiciales y las controversias pueden cambiar.
▾ Assessment — State of Institutional Independence in Spain (March 2026)

Overview

Of the 45 institutions tracked in this monitor, only 2 show no evidence of political capture (decay 0), while 8 exhibit clear capture (decay 3) and a further 15 show significant colonisation (decay 2). The average independence score across all institutions is 42.6 out of 100 — well below the midpoint, and below the threshold typically associated with effective institutional autonomy in comparative governance literature. Twenty-nine of the 45 positions are currently held by individuals who previously served as ministers, secretaries of state, or senior political appointees of the same government that named them. That is, 64% of the institutions tracked are led by former officials of the executive branch that appointed them.

The picture that emerges is not one of sporadic political interference, but of a systematic and structurally embedded pattern of institutional colonisation that spans the constitutional, judicial, regulatory, media, and public enterprise spheres simultaneously.

The Constitutional Tier: The Most Compromised Layer

The most alarming finding concerns the constitutional institutions — the bodies specifically designed by Spain's 1978 Constitution to act as counterweights to executive power. This sector records the lowest average independence score of any category: 21.8 out of 100, with an average capture level of 2.4 out of 3. Three of the five constitutional institutions tracked score in the single digits.

The Constitutional Court is led by a president (Cándido Conde-Pumpido, indScore: 8) who founded the progressive judicial association Jueces para la Democracia, served as Attorney General under the Zapatero government, and was elected by the progressive bloc installed by the Sánchez government. He is under criminal investigation by the Supreme Court — the first sitting Constitutional Court president in Spanish democratic history to face such proceedings. His vice-president (Inmaculada Montalbán, indScore: 7) publicly acknowledged PSOE membership during her Senate confirmation hearing and subsequently voted in favour of the amnesty law that benefited the very politician who had awarded her a regional medal of honour. Under this leadership, the Court has validated the Amnesty Law, repeatedly blocked opposition initiatives, and behaved, in the assessment of multiple constitutional scholars, as a political instrument of the governing coalition rather than as an independent arbiter of constitutionality.

The Council of State — Spain's supreme consultative body, tasked with issuing independent legal opinions on government legislation — is led by Carmen Calvo (indScore: 6), a PSOE member of five parliamentary terms, former Deputy Prime Minister and cabinet minister under Sánchez, who was a sitting Member of Parliament until days before her appointment. She is the second consecutive former socialist minister to be placed in this role after the first (Magdalena Valerio) was annulled by the Supreme Court. The body charged with reviewing the legality of government bills is therefore led by a career politician of the governing party.

The Ombudsman was until recently a three-time PSOE candidate for the Presidency of the Community of Madrid, whose appointment was interpreted by commentators across the political spectrum as a dignified exit from a failed electoral career rather than an institutional choice. The role requires a 3/5 parliamentary majority and the PP's agreement was secured, providing some democratic legitimacy — but the precedent of placing an active politician in a watchdog role within months of electoral defeat is structurally corrosive.

The Revolving Door: From Cabinet to Regulator

The most pervasive mechanism of capture documented in this monitor is the direct transition of serving or recently departed government ministers and senior officials into the leadership of ostensibly independent institutions, with no cooling-off period, no competitive process, and no parliamentary ratification.

The clearest cases follow a consistent template. José Luis Escrivá (indScore: 11) served as a cabinet minister for four years under Sánchez — including publicly criticising the Bank of Spain's analyses of his own pension reform — and was then appointed Governor of the Bank of Spain, the institution he had previously attacked, breaking a 45-year bipartisan convention. Beatriz Corredor (indScore: 12), a former Zapatero minister and PSOE regional candidate, was placed in charge of Red Eléctrica de España, the operator of the national electricity market, during the worst energy price crisis in Spain's recent history, while her government was simultaneously intervening politically in that same market. Raquel Sánchez (indScore: 8), Minister of Transport until 2023, was appointed President of Paradores de Turismo — the state's network of luxury heritage hotels — with no intervening period and no competitive process. Inés Olóndriz became President of AIReF, the independent fiscal watchdog, the day after she left her role as a senior official of the very Ministry of Finance whose accounts AIReF is mandated to scrutinise.

This pattern — cabinet minister or high official to institutional presidency, directly and without process — is not an exception. It is the operating model. It is documented across the Bank of Spain, AIReF, Red Eléctrica, Paradores, SEPI, Renfe, ADIF, Aena, ICO, Enresa, Puertos del Estado, and the Council of State. The cumulative effect is the transformation of independent oversight bodies and state enterprises into extensions of the executive's personnel policy.

The Statistics and Information Layer

Two institutions that produce the factual baseline for public and political debate deserve particular attention. The CIS (Centre for Sociological Research, indScore: 4) is the most extensively documented case of capture in this monitor. Its president has been a PSOE member since 1973, sat on the party's federal executive until days before his appointment, and has produced polling systematically biased in favour of the governing left across every regional election during his tenure — always in the same direction, always benefiting the parties of the governing coalition. He faces criminal charges for misuse of public funds. No other democracy in Western Europe has a sitting party official directing its official polling centre.

The INE (National Statistics Institute, indScore: 52) presents a subtler but structurally important concern. In 2023 it revised upward Spain's entire GDP historical series, improving the debt-to-GDP ratio just before the general election. The revision may have been methodologically defensible, but the timing and the absence of any independent oversight of INE's methodological decisions — it is answerable to the Ministry of Economic Affairs whose figures it produces — means there is no institutional check on such interventions.

The Media Ecosystem

Spain's public media landscape is fully captured. RTVE, the national broadcaster with the widest reach in Spain, is led by a president elected by the bloc of Sánchez's parliamentary allies over PP objections, and has faced repeated internal complaints from its own editorial council about systematic editorial bias. Agencia EFE (indScore: 22), the world's largest Spanish-language news agency and the primary source for hundreds of media outlets in Spain and Latin America, is presided over by a political journalist appointed by direct government decree with no independent process, giving the executive a global-reach information lever that is rarely discussed in analyses of media capture. The combined effect on the information environment is significant: the government controls the framing of news at the point of production, not just at the point of distribution.

The Judicial and Prosecutorial Chain

The independence of the judiciary is formally stronger than the constitutional or executive tier, but faces a structural vulnerability: the governing body of the judiciary (CGPJ) and the Attorney General's office are both ultimately dependent on political appointment processes. Spain has had three successive Attorneys General under the Sánchez government, none appointed with opposition consensus. The most recent predecessor was criminally convicted for leaking private data about a political opponent. The current Fiscalía Anticorrupción — whose mandate is to prosecute political corruption — was appointed by the same Attorney General chain of command, creating a structural conflict of interest that is impossible to resolve within the current institutional design.

What Genuine Independence Looks Like

The monitor also identifies the exceptions, which are instructive precisely because they are so few. The Nuclear Safety Council (indScore: 72) is the most technically independent regulator in this dataset — its president has a career built entirely within the institution, it reports to Parliament rather than the government, and its technical reports have on occasion contradicted official government policy on nuclear plant closures. The Prado Museum (indScore: 78) selected its current director through an internal competitive process supervised by its broad-based board of trustees. The National Library (indScore: 85) is led by a director who has served across governments of opposite political sign without being replaced — the clearest possible signal of genuine institutional independence. These three cases share a common structure: an appointment mechanism that limits direct government discretion, a technical career track within the institution itself, and a record of decisions that occasionally diverge from government preference.

Structural Assessment

The data supports a finding that goes beyond individual appointments: Spain's system of institutional governance has a design flaw that has been exploited to structural completion. The legal framework governing most of these institutions allows — and in some cases requires — direct executive appointment with minimal or no external validation. In the absence of political culture norms that discourage such appointments (norms that operated informally during earlier democratic periods), the legal permissiveness translates directly into systematic capture.

The problem is not primarily one of individual moral failure. Most of the people documented here are competent professionals. The problem is that a system designed with insufficient structural safeguards, operating under a government willing to use every legal margin available to it, produces capture at scale. The institutions most affected — the Constitutional Court, the Council of State, the Attorney General's office, the Bank of Spain, the national broadcaster, the national polling centre — are precisely those whose independence matters most for the quality of Spanish democracy.

In comparative European terms, the breadth of simultaneous capture across constitutional, judicial, regulatory, and media institutions documented here is unusual for a consolidated Western democracy. It is more commonly associated with the early stages of democratic backsliding in Central and Eastern European contexts. Whether Spain is experiencing a temporary episode linked to a particular governing coalition's practices, or a more durable degradation of its institutional culture, will depend in large part on whether successor governments of any political colour reverse the precedents being set, or adopt and extend them.
