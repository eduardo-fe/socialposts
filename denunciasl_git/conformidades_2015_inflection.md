# The 2015–2016 Inflection in *Condenas con Conformidad*: What the Data Show and Why

## Reading the Numbers First

Before attributing causes, it is worth being precise about what the data actually show. *Condenas con conformidad* hovered in a narrow band between roughly 15,700 and 16,700 from 2007 to 2015 — effectively flat for eight years, with a modest trough in 2012–2013 that tracks the broader contraction in *denuncias* during those years of economic crisis. The break is not actually visible in 2015 itself, which records 16,303 — squarely within the historical range. It is in **2016** that the series lurches upward to 19,090, a jump of nearly 17 percent in a single year that has no precedent in the dataset. From there the series accelerates continuously: 20,329 in 2017, 22,451 in 2018, 24,294 in 2019, and onward to 39,234 by 2025. The 2016 break is the real event; the 2015 figure is the last observation in the old regime.

This timing matters enormously for interpretation, because 2015 was one of the most legislatively busy years in the history of Spain's gender violence framework. Laws enacted throughout that year came into force in the second half of it — some as late as October — and would therefore have produced their first meaningful statistical fingerprint in the 2016 data rather than in 2015. What happened in that legislative year is consequently the most promising place to look for explanations.

## The Legislative Cluster of 2015

Four distinct pieces of legislation, enacted between March and October 2015, restructured the institutional and substantive environment in ways that would predictably raise the volume of *conformidades*.

**LO 1/2015 (Penal Code reform, in force 1 July 2015)** was the most significant. It made two changes directly relevant to the *conformidad* count. First, it abolished the category of *faltas* — minor criminal infractions — and replaced them with a new tier of *delitos leves* (minor offences). For gender violence, this was not a mere terminological adjustment. The old *faltas* of light threats (*amenazas leves*) and light coercion (*coacciones leves*) committed by a male partner against a female partner had already been converted into full *delitos* by LO 1/2004, but other harassment-adjacent conduct remained in the falta register. The 2015 reform swept the remaining borderline conduct fully into the *delito leve* category, all of which fell under the jurisdiction of the *Juzgados de Violencia sobre la Mujer* and all of which was eligible to be resolved by *conformidad*. In other words, the universe of acts that could produce a *condena con conformidad* expanded in mid-2015. Second, LO 1/2015 introduced the new offence of *stalking* (Article 172 ter of the Penal Code), criminalising repeated harassment, surveillance, and unwanted contact for the first time as a standalone crime rather than as a sub-category of coercion. The aggravated form under Article 172 ter.2 applied explicitly where the victim was or had been the defendant's intimate partner — a gender violence context — and was immediately cognisable by the JVM. Stalking was not merely a new label; it captured a category of conduct, extremely common in the post-separation phase of abusive relationships, that had previously either gone uncharged (because no precise offence existed) or had been shoehorned uncomfortably into coercion or breach-of-protection-order charges. Making it explicitly triable before a specialist court with a *conformidad* pathway substantially increased the pool of cases capable of ending in a plea conviction.

**Ley 4/2015 (Estatuto de la Víctima, in force 28 October 2015)** was Spain's transposition of EU Directive 2012/29, which established minimum standards for victim rights across the criminal justice system. For gender violence cases the law had several provisions that are likely to have nudged the *conformidad* rate upward over time, even if their effect was diffuse rather than immediate. It mandated systematic notification of victims about every key procedural development, including charging decisions and sentencing outcomes. It required individual needs-assessment for victims before hearings, with corresponding protection measures. And it created a nationwide network of *Oficinas de Asistencia a las Víctimas* with obligations to provide early, sustained legal and psychological support. The practical effect was to give more women more preparation before the judicial phase of proceedings, with better-resourced legal representation and a clearer understanding of the process. A victim who understands what a *conformidad* means — that the defendant is accepting guilt without the need for her to testify in open court — is arguably more likely to support an outcome that relies on it. Conversely, better legal advice from the outset may have pushed prosecutors and defence lawyers toward earlier negotiation of *conformidades*, since a well-supported victim is a more credible prosecution witness and therefore makes a contested defence less attractive.

**LO 7/2015 (reform of the LOPJ, in force 1 October 2015)** clarified and expanded the jurisdictional competence of the *Juzgados de Violencia sobre la Mujer* by attributing to them the instruction of *quebrantamiento de condena* (breach of a protection or restraining order) offences arising in gender violence contexts. Previously there had been genuine ambiguity about whether breach-of-order prosecutions were instructed by the JVM or by ordinary investigating courts, and in practice many were handled by the latter. The 2015 LOPJ reform resolved this in favour of the JVM, bringing a class of cases — breach, typically followed by further violence — firmly within the specialist system and thereby back into the *conformidad* statistical record. Breach offences under Article 468 of the Penal Code are procedurally simple cases with high rates of guilty pleas precisely because the breach is usually undeniable; absorbing them into the JVM's caseload would mechanically raise the JVM's *conformidad* count.

**LO 8/2015 (child protection reform)** amended LO 1/2004 to formally recognise minor children of gender violence victims as co-victims in their own right. While its direct contribution to the *conformidad* count is less clear-cut, it added new aggravating circumstances — the presence of children during violence, for instance — that made the base sentence higher and therefore made a *conformidad* at a negotiated level more attractive to defendants wishing to reduce their exposure.

## A Contextual Factor: The Istanbul Convention

In the background to all of this was Spain's ratification in 2014, and entry into force in August of that year, of the Council of Europe's Istanbul Convention (formally: the Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence). The Convention imposed obligations on its signatories to criminalise a specific list of behaviours — psychological violence, stalking, forced marriage, female genital mutilation — with particular requirements around criminalisation of stalking that Spain was already obliged to meet by mid-2015. LO 1/2015's stalking offence was in part a direct response to this obligation. More broadly, ratification created political and institutional pressure to demonstrate systematic prosecution of the Convention's required offences, which reinforced the trend toward case resolution within the specialist system rather than diversion or discontinuation.

## What the Flat Period Before 2015 Reflects

Understanding why *conformidades* were stagnant for eight years is as revealing as understanding why they rose. The 2009–2015 flat period coincides with two overlapping phenomena. First, the economic crisis and the austerity budgets imposed on the justice system from 2011 onwards reduced the resources available to the JVM network, with court staff frozen or cut and the *Oficinas de Asistencia a las Víctimas* operating at reduced capacity. A constrained system tends toward stasis. Second, and probably more importantly, this was a period in which the substantive and procedural architecture of LO 1/2004 was operating without significant expansion of its scope. The crimes eligible for processing through the JVM had not changed since the original law; the procedural incentives had not changed; and the support infrastructure available to victims at the point of decision-making had not materially improved. *Conformidades* were roughly as numerous as the caseload and the incentive structure could sustain, with no new supply of cases and no new reasons for defendants to plead rather than contest.

## Separating the Mechanisms

Three distinct mechanisms likely contributed to the 2016 rise, and they operate at different levels of the system.

The first is **case supply expansion**. The abolition of *faltas* and the introduction of stalking added cases to the JVM that had not previously been there. These new cases entered a system already optimised to resolve gender violence cases via *conformidad*, and would therefore have fed through into the *conformidad* count relatively quickly.

The second is **procedural pull**. The LOPJ amendment bringing breach-of-order cases fully under JVM jurisdiction transferred a large volume of procedurally straightforward cases — already in the system but previously outside the JVM — into a court where *conformidad* is the dominant resolution pathway.

The third is **victim support strengthening**. The *Estatuto de la Víctima* improved the institutional environment for early, informed decision-making by victims and their legal representatives. This is a slower-acting mechanism than the first two, but it would have begun influencing case resolution patterns from late 2015 onward, with its full effect becoming visible in the 2016 statistics.

## What the *Sin Conformidad* Series Does Not Show

A notable feature of the data is that *condenas sin conformidad* — contested convictions — did not increase at anywhere near the same rate during this period. In 2016 it actually fell slightly from 2015 (12,142 vs 12,567), even as *conformidades* surged. This is consistent with the interpretation that the new cases entering the system through stalking and *delito leve* expansion were being resolved by plea rather than contested: they are the kind of cases — harassment, threatening messages, repeated contact — where the factual evidence tends to be documentary and unambiguous, making defence at trial unlikely to succeed and making defendants more amenable to negotiating a reduced sentence. Contested trials, by contrast, are more likely in cases of serious physical violence where the defendant disputes the account. The divergence between the two conviction series is therefore not a puzzle but a confirmation: it is exactly what one would expect if new, plea-suitable offences were being absorbed into the system at scale.

## The Post-2019 Acceleration

It is worth noting briefly that the rise in *conformidades* accelerated again sharply from 2021 onward — 27,809 in 2021, 30,860 in 2022, 35,256 in 2023, 39,234 in 2025 — at rates that exceed what the 2015 reforms alone would predict. This second acceleration is likely driven by a distinct set of causes: the 2017 Pacto de Estado contra la Violencia de Género (a cross-party agreement that injected substantial new funding into the specialist court system and victim support services), the continuing rise in *denuncias* from 2016 onward following the #MeToo period, and possibly the expansion of offences under LO 4/2023 (*Ley trans*) which extended the personal scope of LO 1/2004. But these belong to a different chapter of the story. The structural inflection of 2016 is where the eight-year stasis ended and the long upward trend began, and its proximate causes are rooted almost entirely in the legislative cluster of 2015.
