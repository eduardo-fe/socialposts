# Spain's System for Prosecuting Gender-Based Violence: Architecture, Advantages, and Tensions

## A Purpose-Built Legal Order

Spain's approach to intimate partner violence is not simply a tougher version of ordinary criminal law. It is, rather, a self-contained legal ecosystem built on a single foundational premise: that violence by a man against his female partner or ex-partner is categorically different from other violence, because it is an expression of domination rooted in gender inequality and not merely a private conflict that happens to turn physical. That premise was codified in the *Ley Orgánica de Medidas de Protección Integral contra la Violencia de Género* (LO 1/2004), enacted under the Zapatero government, and it has shaped every institutional arrangement that followed.

The architecture is genuinely distinctive. Dedicated courts — the *Juzgados de Violencia sobre la Mujer* (JVM) — handle both the criminal prosecution and the related civil matters (custody, restraining orders, divorce) for the same case, before the same judge. Ordinary criminal courts do not touch these cases. The definition is deliberately narrow: the law applies to violence perpetrated by a man against a woman who is or has been his intimate partner, regardless of whether they cohabit. Violence in same-sex relationships, violence by women against men, or violence by family members outside the intimate partner context fall outside the scope of this law and are handled under separate domestic violence provisions. This specificity is not accidental. It reflects a legislative theory — contested but deliberate — that the kind of violence captured by the law has a particular social aetiology that justifies a particular institutional response.

The statistics that flow from this system are themselves an artefact of the design. *Denuncias* (formal reports), *condenas con conformidad* (convictions by plea agreement), *condenas sin conformidad* (convictions after a contested trial), and acquittals are all tracked annually and published by the *Consejo General del Poder Judicial*. This granularity is unusual even by European standards. France and Germany publish counts of victims recorded by police, and England and Wales publish prosecution and conviction figures, but none of them produces a continuous, consistent series that splits convictions by procedural route in the way Spain does.

## What the System Does Well

The most obvious advantage is visibility. Because every report, every prosecution, and every verdict passes through a designated institutional channel, the data are comprehensive in a way that diffuse, multi-agency systems elsewhere struggle to achieve. When Spain reports 199,000 *denuncias* in 2023, that number reflects a single, coherent counting methodology applied consistently across the country. When France reports 271,000 victims recorded by police in the same year, the figure is built from aggregated police and gendarmerie databases with their own definitional inconsistencies. The Spanish number is not necessarily smaller because less violence occurs there; it is smaller in part because it captures a narrower category — male-on-female intimate partner violence only, in a country with roughly 48 million people compared to France's 68 million.

The concentration of expertise in dedicated courts is a second significant advantage. JVM judges develop specialist knowledge of coercive control, the dynamics of recantation, and the evidentiary challenges that arise when a victim's statement is the primary evidence. Support services — psychological, legal, and social — are organised around the court system, so a woman who files a report enters a more coherent institutional pathway than one who reports to a general police station and ends up in a queue for an ordinary criminal court. The *orden de protección* (emergency protection order) can be issued within seventy-two hours of a report, and the judge who issues it is the same judge who will later try any prosecution, which creates continuity unavailable in systems where protective and punitive proceedings are handled by separate courts.

The *conformidad* mechanism — roughly analogous to a plea agreement — serves a further practical function. Because the defendant can accept a conviction and a reduced sentence at an early stage, many cases are resolved quickly without requiring the victim to testify at trial. This matters enormously in a context where victim recantation is one of the principal reasons prosecutions collapse. When a conviction is secured *con conformidad*, the victim is, in effect, protected from having to publicly confront the perpetrator in open court. The data bear out the practical dominance of this route: in 2023, roughly 35,000 convictions were obtained by conformidad against around 13,000 after a contested trial — a ratio that has been broadly stable for years and reflects the strong incentive structure the law creates for early resolution.

## Where the System Creates Problems

Yet the same features that give Spain's system its coherence also generate genuine tensions, some legal and some political.

The most persistent legal challenge is the *constitutionality of asymmetry*. From the outset, critics argued that treating identical acts differently depending on the gender of the perpetrator and victim violated the constitutional principle of equality before the law (Article 14 of the Spanish Constitution). The *Tribunal Constitucional* upheld the law in 2008, but the debate has never fully closed. Over the years, dozens of lower-court judges raised questions of unconstitutionality through the *cuestión de inconstitucionalidad* mechanism, and while most were dismissed, the volume of challenges reflects a genuine unease within the judiciary. The issue was sharpened by the *Ley trans* (LO 4/2023), which extended the protection of the *violencia de género* framework to trans women in heterosexual partnerships — a logical extension under the law's own internal logic, but one that foregrounded the question of how gender is defined for legal purposes in a way the 2004 text had not been required to address.

Second, the narrow scope of the law means that violence outside its definition falls into a less well-resourced legal category. A man who is severely abused by his female partner, or a woman abused by her female partner, is covered by the broader *violencia doméstica* provisions (Article 173 of the Penal Code), which do not benefit from the dedicated court system, the mandatory support services, or the same prosecutorial priority. Critics — including some feminist legal scholars, not just conservative opponents of the law — argue that this creates a hierarchy of victims in which some people's suffering is institutionally valorised more than others'. The counter-argument is that resources are finite and the law directs them where the statistical risk is concentrated; the data on femicides and severe violence are consistent in showing that women in heterosexual relationships face the highest exposure. But the structural asymmetry remains a live normative question.

Third, the *conformidad* mechanism, for all its practical utility, produces its own distortions. Because the system is designed to incentivise early guilty pleas, it creates pressure on defendants to accept convictions even in cases where the evidence is genuinely contested or where the relationship between the parties is more complex than the binary aggressor-victim frame suggests. The *inocentes* figure in the Spanish data — the number of cases in which defendants are acquitted or proceedings dropped — has consistently run at approximately 75 to 80 percent of total *denuncias*, a number that is sometimes cited in public debate as evidence that the law is abused or that false reports are widespread. This inference is almost certainly wrong: the great majority of cases that do not end in a conviction involve insufficient evidence, victim withdrawal, or procedural discontinuation — not demonstrated fabrication — and the same pattern appears in comparable jurisdictions. But the raw figure is politically legible in ways that the more nuanced methodological explanation is not, and it has been weaponised by critics of the law in ways that can themselves discourage reporting.

Finally, the very success of the law in generating data creates its own political problem. Year-on-year increases in *denuncias* — from roughly 125,000 in 2007 to over 200,000 by 2024 — are almost certainly driven partly by improved reporting propensity, better institutional reception of complaints, and a broader social shift in the willingness of women to report. But they are routinely reported in public discourse as increases in violence itself, which creates a false picture of a society in crisis and can trigger policy responses calibrated to the measured figure rather than to underlying incidence. Spain is not unique in this; France and England and Wales face the same interpretive challenge. But because Spain's data are more granular and more consistently tracked, the year-on-year comparison is more salient and the misinterpretation harder to correct.

## A Model Worth Studying, Not Simply Copying

None of these problems undermines the case for Spain's model; they are the inherent tensions of any attempt to use law to address a phenomenon that is at once deeply particular and structurally general. What the Spanish system demonstrates is that the choice to build a dedicated institutional apparatus — rather than relying on general criminal law applied with sensitivity — does produce measurable gains: earlier intervention, better data, more coherent victim support pathways, and a prosecutorial culture in which gender-based violence is treated as a public matter rather than a private one. What it also demonstrates is that those gains come with costs in legal symmetry, resource allocation across different categories of victims, and the political management of statistics that are simultaneously the system's greatest asset and its most easily misread output. Other countries looking at their own — typically higher and more broadly defined — figures might conclude that Spain's trade-offs are worth making. They should also be clear about what exactly they would be buying.
