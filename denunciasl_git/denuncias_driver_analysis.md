# What Is Driving the Rise in Denuncias — and What the Data Cannot Tell Us

## The Problem

The data presents a structural puzzle. *Denuncias* per 100,000 rose 18% between 2006 and 2025. *Condenas con conformidad* per 100,000 more than doubled (+116%). *Condenas sin conformidad* per 100,000 are flat (+1%). The gap — the share of *denuncias* producing no conviction — sits persistently between 74% and 83%, with no trend. The conviction rate per *denuncia* is flat at 22–25% throughout.

There are two fundamentally different interpretations of this picture, and they have opposite policy implications:

**Interpretation A — Falling cost of denouncing:** The rise in *denuncias* reflects that more women who previously endured violence in silence are now reporting it. The gap is large because the system was always unable to prosecute most cases, and a larger absolute number of non-convictions is the expected arithmetic consequence of higher reporting. This interpretation is broadly positive: the law is working as intended, and the gap measures the pre-existing limits of the criminal justice system, not a new failure.

**Interpretation B — Composition change:** The rise in *denuncias* is substantially driven by legislative changes that inflated the filing count without a corresponding increase in underlying violence — new offence categories, derivative filings, definitional expansion. The gap is large and growing because the new cases entering the system are structurally harder to prosecute. This interpretation is more troubling for the headline statistics, because it means the *denuncias* series is an increasingly unreliable measure of what it is supposed to measure.

The honest conclusion of this analysis is that **the available aggregate data cannot clearly distinguish between these two interpretations**, and that a previous version of this document made a logical error in claiming otherwise. That error and its implications are corrected below.

---

## The Corrected Argument

An earlier draft argued that the stable ~70% victim-filed share of *denuncias* was a control variable that weakened the falling-cost hypothesis. The argument was: if falling cost were the main driver, you would expect the victim-filed share to rise as previously-silent women came forward. Its stability supposedly showed that victim behaviour had not changed dramatically.

This is wrong. The stable share tells you nothing of the kind.

If *denuncias* per 100,000 rose from 361 to 427 — an increase of 66 per 100,000 — and the victim-filed share stayed at 70%, then victim-filed *denuncias* per 100,000 rose by approximately 46 per 100,000. That is not stability. That is the dominant component of the entire increase. The stable share does not neutralise the growth; it distributes it. Victim-filed *denuncias* grew at the same rate as total *denuncias*, which is entirely consistent with falling cost having operated equally across all filing routes. The data on filing shares is simply silent on whether the cost fell; it only tells you that whatever drove the increase affected all three sources — victim, police *ex-officio*, third party — proportionally. A broad reduction in filing costs is an entirely plausible explanation for that pattern.

The consequence of correcting this error is significant: **the falling-cost hypothesis becomes considerably harder to falsify from the data we have.** We need to be precise about what we can and cannot conclude.

---

## Four Mechanisms That Are Certainly Contributing

Whatever the relative weight of falling cost and composition change, four mechanisms are operating in the data and can be identified with reasonable confidence.

**Composition shift from LO 1/2015.** The Penal Code reform introduced stalking (*acoso*, Art. 172 ter) as a standalone offence for the first time in July 2015, and replaced *faltas* with *delitos leves*, absorbing conduct previously tracked in a different register. Neither change involved new violence; both involved the same or similar conduct now generating a countable *denuncia* where previously it generated nothing or a record outside the JVM system. The 2016 inflection in the *denuncias* series — a 10% jump between 2015 and 2016 — is at least partly this mechanical absorption.

**Derivative inflation from *quebrantamiento*.** This is the most structurally important mechanism and the least visible in public commentary. According to the *Fiscalía General del Estado*'s 2024 *Memoria*, 22% of all prosecutions in 2023 were for *quebrantamiento de medida cautelar o condena* — breach of a restraining or protection order. These are not original acts of violence. They are derivative filings that only exist because a prior *denuncia* was filed and a protection order issued. One relationship with repeated breaches generates multiple entries in the *denuncias* count. Before LO 7/2015, *quebrantamiento* cases were largely instructed by ordinary courts outside the JVM statistical system. After 2015, they migrated entirely into the gender violence register. The *denuncias* count is therefore not a count of violent relationships or violent incidents; it is a count of legal filings, and the denominator of the gap calculation has been inflated by a category that is, by construction, a second or third count of the same underlying situation.

**Reporting propensity — genuinely operating, magnitude unknown.** The cultural shift associated with #MeToo from 2017, the *Pacto de Estado* of 2017, improved police reception of complaints, mandatory legal representation from the moment of filing, and the *Estatuto de la Víctima* of 2015 all reduced the personal and institutional cost of filing. This mechanism is real and supported by qualitative evidence. The aggregate data cannot tell us how large its contribution is relative to the composition effects above. Critically, as established in the corrected argument, the stable victim-filed share cannot be used as evidence against this mechanism.

**Definition expansion.** The scope of LO 1/2004 has been progressively widened by successive legislation and judicial interpretation. LO 8/2015 made children of victims co-victims with independent standing. LO 10/2022 brought structural changes to sexual violence law. Each expansion adds a category of situations now generating *denuncias* within the gender violence register that previously went through different channels.

## What the Data Can Still Tell Us

After correcting the fallacy, the falling-cost hypothesis is harder to falsify with the data we have. But three tests remain available, with varying force.

**Withdrawal rate.** If falling cost brought more reluctant or ambivalent filers into the system, the rate of victim withdrawal (*dispensa* under Art. 416 LECrim) before trial should rise, since marginal filers are more likely to reconsider. The CGPJ data shows withdrawal running at approximately 9% of cases in early 2024 and *falling* — down 16% year-on-year. This weakly contradicts the falling-cost hypothesis as a sole driver. However, it is confounded: the *Estatuto de la Víctima* of 2015 substantially improved victim support throughout proceedings, which would retain filers who might otherwise have withdrawn, independently of why they filed in the first place. The test is inconclusive.

**Conviction rate per *denuncia*.** If falling cost brings in structurally weaker cases, the conviction rate per *denuncia* should fall. It is flat at 22–25% throughout the entire period. This also weakly contradicts a pure falling-cost story. But it is similarly confounded: new offence types like *quebrantamiento* and stalking may have conviction rates similar to older offences, so composition effects could cancel out in the aggregate. Without offence-type-specific conviction rates over time, this test cannot resolve the question.

**Femicides.** This is the most powerful available test and the one that most clearly points in a direction. Femicides — intimate partner homicides of women — are the hardest possible outcome to underreport or misclassify. If underlying violence were genuinely increasing in line with *denuncias*, femicides should rise. They have not. The femicide rate per 100,000 fell from approximately 0.155 in 2006 to 0.119 in 2023, a decline of 23% per capita, while *denuncias* per 100,000 rose 15% over the same period. The trend slopes are not merely different in magnitude but opposite in sign: femicides have a statistically significant downward trend (slope −0.003/yr, R² = 0.61, p < 0.001) while *denuncias* have a statistically significant upward trend (slope +6.2/yr, R² = 0.49, p < 0.001).

This divergence is the strongest single piece of evidence available from aggregate data. It is most consistent with a situation where underlying severe violence has not increased — and may have slightly decreased — while the *denuncias* count has risen due to a combination of improved reporting of non-lethal violence, composition change, and derivative filing inflation. It does not prove the falling-cost hypothesis; it proves that the rise in *denuncias* does not represent a proportional rise in the most severe end of the violence distribution.

---

## What the Data Cannot Resolve

The falling-cost hypothesis and the composition-change hypothesis are observationally difficult to separate from the aggregate series. Both predict rising *denuncias*. Both predict a roughly stable gap ratio. Both are consistent with a flat conviction rate per *denuncia*. After correcting the filing-share argument, no clean falsification test survives from the data in hand.

The single most important missing dataset is an annual time series of *denuncias* broken down by offence type — *lesiones*, *amenazas*, *quebrantamiento*, *stalking*, *coacciones* — going back to 2006. If *quebrantamiento* as a share of total *denuncias* grew from, say, 8% in 2006 to 22% in 2023, that alone would quantify the derivative inflation effect and put a ceiling on how much of the rise can be attributed to falling cost. If the share of *lesiones* (physical injury with objective medical documentation) fell as a proportion of the total while softer-evidence categories grew, that would be direct evidence of composition dilution.

This data exists. The CGPJ publishes quarterly statistical bulletins from every JVM court broken down by offence type, at provincial and regional level. It has never been compiled into a consistent national annual time series. That compilation — a research task of perhaps a few weeks for a team with access to the CGPJ's statistical portal — would be the single most valuable empirical contribution to understanding what is actually happening to gender violence in Spain. Without it, the debate between the two interpretations will continue to be conducted largely on the basis of prior beliefs rather than evidence.

---

## What Can Be Said with Confidence

The *denuncias* series is an increasingly unreliable proxy for the prevalence of intimate partner violence in Spain. It is a count of legal filings, and the composition of those filings has changed materially since LO 1/2015. The gap between *denuncias* and convictions is large and structurally expected given these changes, but the precise contribution of each mechanism — composition change, derivative inflation, reporting propensity — cannot be quantified from the available data.

What can be said confidently is that the most severe end of the violence distribution — intimate partner homicide — has not risen and has if anything declined per capita. The rise in *denuncias* reflects something real (more women reporting conduct that was previously endured in silence) and something mechanical (a larger and more heterogeneous category of filings counting derivative and lower-severity events). Whether the balance lies more with genuine increased reporting or with legislative and definitional inflation is a question the available aggregate data cannot answer.

The intellectually honest summary is this: the data is consistent with the falling-cost hypothesis, consistent with the composition-change hypothesis, and consistent with any weighted combination of the two. The femicide trend tells us the severe end of the distribution has not worsened. The offence-type composition data that would resolve the rest does not exist in compiled form. Until it does, anyone claiming confident knowledge of which interpretation is correct — in either direction — is reading more into the aggregate series than it can support.
