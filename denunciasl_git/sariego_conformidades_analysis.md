# Coercive Conformidades in Spanish Gender Violence Cases: Assessing the Sariego Morillo Argument

## Reference

Sariego Morillo, J.L. (2021). *Sobre las prácticas abusivas en los Juzgados españoles en las sentencias por conformidad*. Confilegal, 29 March 2021. Available at: https://confilegal.com/20210329-sobre-las-practicas-abusivas-en-los-juzgados-espanoles-en-las-sentencias-por-conformidad/

---

## Summary of the Article

Sariego Morillo, a family law practitioner, argues that the rising rate of *condenas por conformidad* in Spanish gender violence proceedings is not primarily evidence of system effectiveness, but rather of a systematic use of plea-pressure tactics that produce convictions regardless of guilt. His argument has three interlocking components.

**The institutional incentive structure.** The *Fiscalía General del Estado*'s Instrucción 2/2009 formalised a protocol between the prosecution service and the Consejo General de la Abogacía Española specifically to promote *conformidades* as a caseload management tool. The FGE's own *Memorias* describe the mechanism as a "balón de oxígeno" for a system under pressure. The CGPJ Observatorio and the Delegación del Gobierno have, in Sariego's account, explicitly framed rising *conformidad* rates as a success indicator rather than a management convenience. The institutional pressure runs, he argues, in a single direction: towards more pleas, not towards more accurate justice.

**The overcharging mechanism.** Prosecutors routinely aggregate multiple charges from a single incident — *lesiones*, *amenazas*, *coacciones*, plus aggravating circumstances such as the presence of children — producing a combined sentence request that, if the defendant were convicted of all counts at trial, would result in imprisonment. This aggregate charge creates the pressure from which a reduced *conformidad* offer provides relief. The defendant, typically without prior criminal record and therefore unfamiliar with the justice system, faces a choice between accepting a suspended sentence now or risking several years' imprisonment at trial. Many accept the former even when innocent. Sariego claims, anecdotally, that women are sometimes coached at municipal support centres to add supplementary details — children present, verbal threats, psychological intimidation — that amplify the charge sheet beyond the facts initially reported.

**The public defender fee structure.** Duty solicitors (*abogados del Turno de Oficio*) receive approximately €135 per expediente regardless of whether the case is resolved by *conformidad* or goes to full trial. Since a *conformidad* can be concluded in minutes while a contested trial may require tens of hours of preparation, the fee structure creates a rational incentive for defence lawyers to advise acceptance of a plea, independent of the merits. Sariego does not present this as individual corruption but as a predictable structural consequence of a compensation model that does not reward effort.

He concludes with a series of questions — not answered — about whether *conformidades* are being used to inflate conviction statistics, who benefits from that inflation, and whether the system's apparent success in reducing gender violence is real or largely statistical artefact.

## Assessing the Claims

Sariego's argument contains elements that are well-founded, elements that are structurally plausible but anecdotally supported, and elements that are rhetorically overstated. These need to be disaggregated.

### What is verifiable and well-founded

The existence of Instrucción 2/2009 and its content is confirmed from primary sources. The FGE did formally promote *conformidades* in gender violence proceedings as a systemic tool. The description of this as institutional pressure to maximise plea rates rather than to maximise accurate prosecution is a reasonable characterisation of what the instruction does, though the FGE would describe the same mechanism in different terms.

The duty solicitor fee structure is documented in the Abogacía Española/Wolters Kluwer study he cites. The asymmetric incentive it creates — same fee, vastly different workload — is a real structural problem that the profession itself has documented and complained about for years. That this creates rational pressure toward advising *conformidad* regardless of case strength is not a serious contestable claim; it follows arithmetically from the fee structure.

The innocent-plea phenomenon is well-documented in the comparative criminal justice literature. The US Innocence Project, which he cites at the opening, has produced robust evidence that defendants — including innocent ones — accept guilty pleas when the alternative is too risky. There is no principled reason why Spain's system would be structurally immune to this. The *conformidad* mechanism involves the same rational calculus.

The overcharging tactic is visible in the Fiscalía's own guidance on gender violence charging. The practice of aggregating connected charges from a single incident is not a secret; it is standard prosecutorial procedure. That it creates conformidad pressure is a consequence, not a conspiracy.

### What is plausible but not evidenced

The claim that municipal women's centres coach complainants to add details not present in the initial report is asserted on the basis of professional hearsay — conversations with colleagues and WhatsApp groups. It may occur in individual cases. It cannot be established as a systematic practice on the evidence presented. The absence of initial details that later appear in proceedings is a real evidentiary issue in these cases, but has multiple explanations besides coaching: trauma affects recall, victims may initially minimise, and interview techniques can elicit fuller accounts over time. Sariego's explanation is one possibility among several.

### What is overstated or analytically imprecise

The comparison to totalitarian regimes is rhetorical excess and does not advance the analytical argument. More substantively, Sariego conflates three distinct situations throughout the piece — a system that creates incentives toward *conformidad* regardless of guilt; a system that engineers false complaints; and a system that achieves convictions of genuinely guilty defendants through legitimate pressure. These are not the same thing, and the failure to distinguish them allows the piece to slide between "structural problem" and "systematic injustice" without establishing the latter.

The 4.7% figure for "sentencias recurribles tras juicio debido" is presented without a definition of what "recurrible" means in his usage or a methodology for deriving it. Without this it cannot be evaluated.

## What the Article Adds to the Analytical Framework

Read alongside the quantitative analysis of the CGPJ data, this article contributes a specific mechanism for the rising *conformidad* ratio that our previous analysis did not fully capture.

We had identified two interpretations of the ratio's rise from 1.68 in 2006 to 3.58 in 2025:

- **Rational defendant behaviour in response to strong evidence:** cases are harder to contest because forensic and digital evidence has improved, and the JVM courts convict 92% of contested cases, making a trial a bad bet.
- **New case types that are plea-suitable by nature:** stalking and breach-of-order cases have documentary evidence that defendants cannot seriously dispute, so they plead.

Sariego adds a third:

- **Irrational (or coerced) defendant behaviour in response to systemic pressure:** defendants plead not because the evidence against them is strong but because they are scared, underrepresented, facing artificially inflated charges, and rationally prefer a certain suspended sentence to a risky trial that might produce imprisonment.

These three interpretations are not mutually exclusive. The dataset of *conformidades* almost certainly contains all three types of defendant. The question — unresolvable from aggregate data — is what the proportions are. A rising ratio is consistent with all three mechanisms operating simultaneously, and the share of each cannot be read off from the headline statistics.

### The conviction rate problem

This is where Sariego's argument has its most significant implication for how we read the data. The CGPJ Observatorio consistently presents the rising conviction rate per sentence — now over 80%, with JVM courts reaching 92% — as evidence of increasing system effectiveness. The Observatorio's president stated in 2023 that a high conviction rate is "a clear symptom that the functioning of justice and all operators who collaborate with judges is improving every day."

This framing is, at minimum, incomplete. The conviction rate per sentence measures the rate at which cases that reach a verdict produce a condena. It measures this accurately. What it does not measure is whether the *conformidades* within that count accurately track guilt. If a substantial share of *conformidades* involve defendants who accepted a plea for structural reasons unrelated to their actual culpability — overcharging pressure, inadequate representation, rational risk aversion — then a rising conviction rate per sentence is partly measuring the effectiveness of plea pressure, not the accuracy of the justice system.

The two metrics are only equivalent under the assumption that *conformidad* perfectly tracks guilt. Sariego's argument, and the comparative literature, suggest that assumption is unwarranted. How unwarranted it is — what share of conformidades involve innocent defendants — is genuinely unknowable from the data we have. But the possibility is structurally real and is supported by the design of the system he describes.

### What this does not establish

Sariego's article does not establish, and should not be read as establishing, that the gender violence system in Spain is primarily a machine for producing false convictions. The structural problems he identifies — perverse incentives, overcharging, inadequate defence resources — are real features of the system, but they exist alongside genuine prosecutions of genuinely guilty defendants. The femicide data shows severe violence has not increased and has if anything declined, which is inconsistent with a pure system-failure narrative.

What the article does establish, in combination with the institutional evidence, is that *the conviction rate per sentence cannot be used as a straightforward proxy for system accuracy*. It is a measure of case closure, not of truth. This is a narrower but more defensible claim than the article's headline rhetoric, and it is an important corrective to official statistics presented without qualification.

---

## The Honest Summary

The Sariego Morillo article is most valuable when read as a structural critique rather than a conspiracy theory. Its three core institutional observations — the FGE protocol promoting pleas, the asymmetric duty solicitor fee structure, and the overcharging tactic — are all documented or documentable. Together they describe a system that has rational incentives to produce *conformidades* independently of their correspondence to guilt.

The leap from "the system has incentives to produce pleas" to "the system is systematically convicting innocents" is not established by the evidence presented, and the article's rhetorical register makes it easy to dismiss the valid structural points along with the overstatements.

The right analytical response is neither to accept the strongest version of his claim nor to dismiss it because of its polemical framing. It is to recognise that the rising *conformidad* series contains, by structural necessity, an unknowable but non-trivial proportion of cases where defendants pleaded guilty for reasons that have nothing to do with their guilt — and that the official statistics do not, and probably cannot, disaggregate this proportion from the rest. This is a limitation of the data that deserves to be stated plainly, rather than obscured by conviction-rate figures presented as unambiguous success indicators.
