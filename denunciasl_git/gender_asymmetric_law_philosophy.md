# The Structural Argument for Gender-Asymmetric Criminal Law and Its Internal Inconsistency

## The Argument You Are Making

The pro-asymmetry argument for LO 1/2004 runs: *the same physical act carries different social meaning depending on who commits it and in what structural context, and criminal law should reflect that social meaning rather than treating identical acts as equivalent.* Male-on-female partner violence is aggravated because it expresses and reinforces a pre-existing structure of domination — the harm is not just the individual act but its participation in a broader pattern of subordination.

Applying that same logic to maternal filicide generates a conclusion the proponents of the original argument would almost certainly reject: if a mother kills her child, she is not merely an individual who commits a homicide — she is the primary structural protector of that child, the person on whom the child depends most completely, the one whose betrayal of that role is therefore the most fundamental possible violation of a structural relationship. By the logic of the gender violence argument, this social meaning should attract an *aggravated* sentence compared with a father who commits the same act.

Almost no one argues for this. And that asymmetry in where the logic is applied — and where it is not — exposes something important.

---

## What the Inconsistency Reveals

The inconsistency is real, but it is worth diagnosing precisely what it shows.

**First diagnosis:** the argument from social meaning is not a principled legal argument at all — it is a post-hoc rationalisation applied selectively to reach conclusions already desired on other grounds. If the argument genuinely were "criminal law should reflect structural social roles and the meaning of acts within them," it would have to be applied consistently. Maternal filicide would attract aggravated penalties. Violence by a carer against an elderly dependent — structurally analogous — would be treated differently depending on whether the carer is the "primary" one. Violence by teachers against pupils might be disaggregated by the intimacy and dependency of the relationship. None of this happens. The argument from social meaning is deployed when it produces the result of protecting women from men, and quietly withdrawn everywhere else.

This diagnosis supports the formal-equality critique most strongly. It suggests that the substantive-equality argument is not doing independent philosophical work — it is providing cover for an outcome driven by a prior political commitment.

**Second diagnosis, more charitable to the original argument:** the inconsistency reveals not that the argument is wrong, but that it is *incomplete*. The specific claim for gender-asymmetric treatment of intimate partner violence is not just that it involves a structural relationship of power — it is that it involves a *historically entrenched, demographically pervasive, and institutionally under-addressed* pattern of harm directed at a specific group. The elements that do the real work are not "structural role" alone but the combination of: prevalence at scale, historical invisibility in the legal system, and systematic disadvantage of the victim group in accessing protection. Female victims of male partner violence meet all three criteria in a way that, the argument goes, justifies legislative intervention.

Maternal filicide does not meet the same combination. It is not pervasive at scale. Mothers who kill children have not been historically protected by institutional indifference or treated as presumptive liars. The child protection system, whatever its failures, is not structured to disbelieve allegations against mothers. The asymmetry in the original argument is therefore not merely "structural role" — it is a more specific constellation of features that produce systematic under-protection of a class of victims.

---

## Why the Second Diagnosis Is Also Incomplete

The problem with the second, more charitable diagnosis is that it shifts the argument to empirical rather than principled ground — and empirical grounds are unstable foundations for criminal law's equality norms.

If the justification for gender-asymmetric punishment is that historically there has been under-protection of female victims of male partner violence, then the justification is contingent on that historical condition persisting. If, in a hypothetical future Spain, institutional responses to domestic violence were fully adequate, reporting rates were equal, conviction rates were comparable across all victim groups, and the social power asymmetry had dissolved — would the asymmetric law still be justified? Most honest proponents would say no. But then the law is not grounded in the *nature* of the act; it is grounded in a claim about current institutional failure. That is a fragile and uncomfortable basis for a permanent structural feature of the criminal code.

More awkwardly still: there are other groups who suffer from persistent under-protection by institutions, systematically skewed conviction rates, and historical invisibility — children being an obvious example, elderly people in care being another. The principle "persistent structural vulnerability justifies asymmetric protection" does not uniquely identify women as intimate partner violence victims as the only group deserving it. Either those other groups also deserve asymmetric protection, or there is something else doing the work in the gender violence argument — something that is not stated openly.

---

## The Deeper Philosophical Fracture

What the filicide example actually exposes is that the "social meaning" argument requires a theory of which structural relationships generate legally relevant moral differences, and that no such theory has been consistently articulated or applied. The argument works by pointing to one dimension of social structure — the gender hierarchy between intimate partners — and treating it as morally salient enough to override formal equality norms in criminal law. But social life contains many structural relationships of dependency, power, and vulnerability, and the law already recognises many of them through aggravated sentencing, special categories of victims, and role-specific duties. The question is not whether structural context ever matters — it clearly does — but whether *sex-of-perpetrator-and-victim* is the right axis to build legal asymmetry around, as opposed to the *relationship type* and *power differential* as such.

A law that aggravated all intimate partner violence, by any combination of parties, where there is evidence of a persistent pattern of coercive control, would capture the social harm the gender violence argument identifies without building sex-based asymmetry into the statute. That such a law has not been passed is a political choice — not the only conclusion that the underlying philosophical argument permits. The filicide example illustrates this perfectly: the normative concern is about betrayals of fundamental protective relationships, not about sex per se. Criminal law could address that without locking in a permanent legal distinction based on the sex of the aggressor.

---

## The Strongest Version of the Challenge

The strongest formulation of the challenge is this: if the argument from structural roles and social meaning is to be taken seriously as a basis for legal asymmetry, it cannot be applied only when it benefits women. Either apply it consistently — which leads to conclusions almost nobody actually wants, including aggravated sentences for mothers who kill — or acknowledge that something else is doing the real work in the gender violence argument, and be honest about what that something is.

The candidates for that "something else" are few. It may be a straightforward majoritarian political preference dressed in philosophical language. It may be a claim that female victimhood specifically, rather than structural vulnerability generally, has a unique historical and symbolic weight that justifies special treatment — a claim that is contestable but at least honest. Or it may be an acknowledgment that law sometimes operates not as a neutral arbiter of individual acts but as a social communication device, and that LO 1/2004 is primarily a statement about the collective rejection of a particular form of oppression rather than a finely calibrated instrument of individual justice.

Each of these is a more defensible position than the structural-roles argument as usually stated — but each also carries costs that its proponents are rarely willing to own. The filicide example is useful precisely because it forces the question into the open: what, exactly, is the principle, and are you prepared to follow it wherever it leads?
